Distributed Systems & the CAP Theorem

Why no distributed system — blockchains included — can ever promise perfect agreement, instant answers, and a network that never breaks, all at once.

What it is

Pick two. The network picks the third for you.

The CAP theorem says a distributed data system can only guarantee two of these three properties at the same time:

C

Consistency

Every node gives back the same, most up-to-date answer — or refuses to answer at all.

A

Availability

Every request gets a real answer, even if it might be a little out of date.

P

Partition tolerance

The system keeps running even when nodes lose the ability to talk to each other.

Here's the catch: at any real scale, network partitions will happen — a cable gets cut, a router fails, a data center loses power. Partition tolerance isn't really a choice you get to opt out of. So the actual decision every distributed system makes is what happens during a partition: stay correct and risk going silent (CP), or stay responsive and risk disagreement (AP).

One more wrinkle

PACELC: the tradeoff that's there even without a partition

CAP only describes what happens during a partition. The PACELC extension points out that even on a perfectly healthy network, you still have to choose between Latency and Consistency: waiting for every replica to confirm a write is safer but slower, while answering immediately from one replica is fast but might be a moment behind.

Where this shows up

CP, AP, and where blockchains fit

  • CP systems — traditional relational databases with synchronous replicas, etcd, Zookeeper. They'd rather return an error than risk an inconsistent answer.
  • AP systems — Cassandra, DynamoDB, CouchDB. They always answer, and reconcile conflicting writes later, often with the same last-write-wins idea used in the simulation above.
  • Blockchains lean AP. When the network briefly disagrees — say, two miners find a block at nearly the same time — different nodes temporarily show different "latest" states. The chain doesn't halt and refuse to answer; it stays available, and the longest-chain (or finality) rule reconciles the fork later. That's exactly why exchanges wait for several confirmations before treating a transaction as settled — they're waiting for eventual consistency to actually arrive.
