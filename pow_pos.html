Consensus Mechanisms (PoW, PoS, BFT…)

How a network of strangers who don't trust each other still agree on a single version of the truth — and what it costs to lie.

What it is

Agreement, without a referee

A consensus mechanism is the rule a decentralized network uses to agree on the next valid block — without any central party deciding for everyone. It's the practical answer to the classic Byzantine Generals Problem: how do mutually distrustful parties, talking over an unreliable network, still coordinate on one shared decision, even if some of them are lying?

Every mechanism answers that question the same general way: make lying expensive, and make honesty the cheapest path to a reward.

Three different bets

What gets put at risk to keep you honest

Proof of Work

Bets real-world computation. Lying means you've wasted electricity racing against everyone else for nothing.

Proof of Stake

Bets real-world money. Lying means your locked-up stake gets destroyed — "slashed."

BFT voting

Bets reputation and explicit votes among known validators. Enough honest votes, and the network finalizes instantly.

Try it: run a round and watch how this mechanism actually picks who proposes the next block.

Real networks rarely run just one of these in its purest form. Delegated PoS adds an election layer on top of staking. Proof of Authority skips the economic bet entirely and trusts known identities. Avalanche and DAG-based designs reach agreement through repeated gossip instead of a single vote or race. They're all still answering the same question this diagram is asking: who gets to propose next, and how does everyone else agree they were right to?

Where this shows up

Same problem, different chains

  • Bitcoin runs Proof of Work — exactly the mining race simulated above, just with real hardware and a much bigger network.
  • Ethereum, Cardano run Proof of Stake — validators are chosen in proportion to stake, and equivocating gets that stake slashed.
  • Hyperledger Fabric, Cosmos's Tendermint run BFT-style voting among a known validator set, finalizing instantly rather than racing.
  • This is exactly why PoW and PoS chains use the fork-choice rule from the last topic — the winner isn't known for certain until enough blocks pile up. BFT chains skip that uncertainty entirely: once two-thirds vote yes, there's no fork to resolve, because there's nothing left to disagree about.
