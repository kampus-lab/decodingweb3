Public-Key Cryptography & Digital Signatures

How "I own this" gets proven on a blockchain — without a password, an ID card, or anyone to ask.

What it is

Two keys, one secret, one shareable

Public-key cryptography gives you a mathematically linked pair of keys. The private key never leaves your device and never gets shared. The public key is meant to be handed out freely — it's how others identify you.

The part that matters for blockchains isn't encryption — it's signing. Sign a message with your private key, and anyone holding your public key can confirm two things at once: that the message really came from whoever holds that private key, and that it hasn't been altered since. No password, no central authority checking IDs — just math.

That's deliberate: in a real wallet, your private key (or the seed phrase it's derived from) is the only thing standing between you and your funds. There's no "forgot password" link. Lose the key, lose access, permanently.

Where this shows up

This is what a wallet actually is

  • Your wallet address is derived from your public key. Anyone can send funds to it; only your private key can authorize spending from it.
  • Every transaction you send is signed exactly like the demo above — the network checks your signature against your public key before accepting it.
  • Validators in Proof-of-Stake sign their votes the same way, so the network can prove afterward exactly who voted for what — and slash the ones who signed conflicting blocks.
