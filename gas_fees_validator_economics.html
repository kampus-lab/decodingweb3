Gas, Fees & Validator Economics: The Market for Block Space

Every block is a scarce resource with a price that sets itself. Watch the auction run live, then see where validators — and the bots circling them — actually make their money.

Why fees exist

Block space is scarce, fees decide who fits

Every block has a hard ceiling on how much computation it can hold, measured in gas — a simple transfer costs a little, a complex contract call costs a lot. Demand to fill that space keeps moving, so the network needs a way to decide who gets in when more people want a seat than there are seats. That mechanism is the fee market, and it has been built two very different ways.

Gas vs. fee: gas is the unit of work a transaction does. The fee is gas used × price per unit of gas — the price is the part that actually moves.

Two ways to price it

First-price auctions vs. base fee + tip

The original model is a pure first-price auction: everyone attaches a fee bid, and whoever builds the next block takes the highest bidders first. You are guessing what everyone else is bidding, which causes overpayment during congestion and stuck transactions during quiet periods.

The modern model — EIP-1559 on Ethereum, and similar designs elsewhere — splits the fee into an algorithmic base fee that adjusts every block based on how full the previous one was, plus an optional tip paid directly to the validator. The base fee is burned, not paid to anyone, so you set a maximum and get refunded the difference instead of guessing a single number.

Legacy auction

  • Simple, no extra infrastructure
  • No reliable signal for the going rate
  • Overpaying and stuck transactions are common

Base fee + tip

  • Price adjusts itself, block by block
  • Refunds the gap between your max and the real fee
  • Tips still spike during genuine demand shocks

Where MEV comes in

Order itself is worth money

Fees are not the only thing on the table — who goes first inside a block can be worth money on its own. This is maximal extractable value, or MEV. The classic case is a sandwich: a bot sees your large swap sitting in the mempool, buys just ahead of you to push the price up, lets your trade execute at the worse price, then sells right after at the price your own trade helped create.

The modern pipeline

Searchers, builders, proposers

Early on, bots fought for ordering by re-bidding gas prices against each other in public, which spiked fees for everyone, not just the bots. That pushed Ethereum toward proposer-builder separation:

Your transaction→ Searchers spot MEV, build bundles→ Builders assemble full blocks→ Proposer picks the highest-paying block→ Block is final

Validator economics

Three layers of income

  • Protocol issuanceNewly issued tokens for proposing and attesting blocks correctly.
  • Priority tipsThe portion of fees users attach for faster inclusion.
  • MEV paymentsWhat builders pay proposers for the right to have their block included, via proposer-builder separation.

Proof-of-work miners earn a block subsidy plus fees too, but carry real electricity and hardware costs — a different cost structure from proof-of-stake validators.
