An explainer

One asset, cut into many digital pieces anyone can own.

Tokenization takes a real asset — a building, a share, a painting, even a tonne of carbon credit — and represents ownership of it with digital tokens on a blockchain. The token is just a wrapper; its value still comes from whatever sits underneath it.

Drag the slider to see why that matters: splitting one expensive asset into many small tokens is what lets ordinary investors buy in.

Two families

Every tokenized asset is either fungible or nonfungible

This single distinction decides how a token behaves: whether each unit is identical and swappable, or one-of-a-kind.

Fungible

Interchangeable units

Every unit is worth exactly the same as every other unit of its kind, so one can stand in for another without any loss of value — the same way one dollar bill is as good as any other.

e.g. currencies · equities · bonds · precious metals
Nonfungible

One-of-a-kind units

Each unit carries its own distinct attributes or value, so no two tokens of the same type are interchangeable — swapping one for another would mean trading away something different.

e.g. lottery tickets · club memberships
Credit side of the ledger

What tokenization is supposed to fix

Seven reasons issuers and investors are drawn to putting assets on-chain.

+

Disintermediation & cost reduction

A blockchain can verify and record ownership on its own, so fewer middlemen are needed for clearing, settlement, and paperwork — and that means lower transaction costs for investors.

+

Increased market participation

Breaking an asset into many small tokens lets people buy a slice instead of the whole thing, opening up real estate, fine art, and private equity to investors who could never meet the usual minimum cheque.

+

Diversification

Once those previously locked-away asset classes become reachable, investors can spread money across categories that tend to move differently from stocks and bonds — and that can also cushion a portfolio against inflation.

+

Increased liquidity

Issuers commonly arrange secondary-market trading for their tokens, and an asset that trades easily tends to be worth more than an otherwise identical asset that's stuck in private hands.

Public assets have traded at a 15–30% premium over private peers — Officer (1997)
+

Faster settlement

A conventional trade can take up to three business days to settle and only moves during banking hours. On-chain, settlement can happen almost the instant a trade is agreed, around the clock — freeing capital faster and letting the asset be reused as collateral sooner.

+

Asset programmability

Because tokens live inside smart contracts, routine post-trade work — paying dividends or coupons, rebalancing a fund — can run automatically on a schedule, trimming the cost of servicing the asset.

+

Transparency & security

Entries written to a blockchain can't quietly be altered or erased by a central authority, and the same ledger is open for anyone to inspect — so every participant sees the same record of who owns what.

Debit side of the ledger

What can go wrong

Tokenization doesn't remove risk — in some ways it adds new kinds.

–

Investment risk

Letting in less experienced retail investors exposes them to asset classes that are inherently more complex than the stocks and bonds they're used to. Less sophisticated investors are also more prone to herd behaviour, which can help inflate speculative bubbles.

–

Regulatory risk

Rules for tokenized assets are still being written, vary widely between countries, and in places barely exist — leaving issuers exposed to penalties, and potentially worse, if regulators later treat the offering differently than expected.

–

Security risk

The blockchain technology behind tokenized assets is still relatively young and can be vulnerable to attacks — and additional weak points can appear wherever it has to connect with older, legacy financial systems.

–

Market integrity risk

Because oversight of this young industry is still thin, problems such as a breach of the blockchain itself or theft of the real-world asset held in custody can go unnoticed longer than they would in more mature, closely monitored markets.

How a deal actually happens

The Security Token Offering, step by step

An STO works much like a traditional securities offering — just with a blockchain doing the bookkeeping.

1

Preliminary discussions

A tokenization platform and a would-be issuer start talking, kicking off the work of shaping an offering investors will eventually see.

2

Structuring

The deal is shaped around the relevant regulatory framework and the format the investment will take — equity, debt, a fund, or some other structure.

3

Subscription

Once listed, investors subscribe. The moment legal ownership passes to them, their subscription money moves to the issuer.

4

Minting & distribution of tokens

With subscriptions settled, tokens are created ("minted") on the chosen blockchain and sent to each investor.

5

Asset servicing

The platform, often acting as custodian, keeps the asset running day to day — paying dividends or coupons, publishing NAV updates, handling ongoing admin.

6

Secondary market trading

The issuer can list the token on a secondary venue, giving holders a way to exit early and giving the asset more liquidity overall.

7

Maturity & burning of tokens

For fixed-term investments — a bond, a closed-end fund — tokens reach maturity, get "burned" out of circulation, and investors receive principal plus return.

Behind the scenes

What an issuer actually has to set up

Three practical jobs sit underneath every tokenized asset, however the offering is marketed.

01 / NETWORK & TOKEN

Register the asset, configure the token

Issuers pick a blockchain (Ethereum, Avalanche…), a token standard suited to the asset (e.g. ERC-20 for fungible tokens, ERC-721/1155 for unique ones), how many tokens to issue, and the rules for minting and burning. They also choose between a permissionless network — open to anyone — and a permissioned one limited to approved participants.

02 / CUSTODY

Store, manage, and distribute tokens

Since a token's value depends entirely on what backs it, the underlying asset is usually held in escrow or with a custodian, with periodic audits confirming the collateral is still all there. Issuers can also list the token on a secondary exchange to make it easier to trade and transfer.

03 / SERVICING

Automate corporate actions

Smart contracts can run the routine business of owning the asset — scheduling NAV updates, paying dividends or coupons on time, keeping records current — without manual work, which lowers the ongoing cost of servicing it.

Is it a security?

The Howey Test, applied to tokens

Whether a tokenized asset has to be registered and disclosed under U.S. securities law comes down to four conditions — all four, or none of it counts.

The test traces back to SEC v. W.J. Howey Co. (1946). Investors bought citrus groves in Florida and immediately leased the land straight back to the Howey Company, which farmed it, sold the fruit, and split the profit with the investors. The SEC argued the arrangement should have been registered as a securities offering — and the Supreme Court agreed, ruling the leaseback deal was an "investment contract."

That ruling still defines what counts as a security today, including for tokenized assets. Toggle the four switches to see how it applies to a token representing one share of Apple stock.

Where this goes next

The upside is clear. The rulebook isn't.

The benefits on both sides of the ledger — lower costs and more automation for issuers, broader access and more liquidity for investors — are why forecasts for this market keep climbing. The thing still holding it back is regulation: rules for tokenized assets remain patchy and inconsistent from one jurisdiction to the next. What's likely needed next is something closer to a shared global vocabulary and a coordinated legal, regulatory, and supervisory framework — one that removes the uncertainty issuers and investors are currently pricing in.
